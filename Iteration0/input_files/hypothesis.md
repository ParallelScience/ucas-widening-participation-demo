The "Conversion Ratio" (High-Tariff/Any HE) currently treats the selective pipeline as a monolithic outcome. I hypothesize that the "High-Tariff Gap" is not merely a function of socioeconomic background, but is significantly mediated by the interaction between specific demographic characteristics (e.g., Ethnicity, SEN) and the "Any HE" baseline. By calculating the **Conditional Selectivity Elasticity**—defined as the sensitivity of the high-tariff progression rate to changes in the overall HE progression rate for each subgroup—we can identify which groups are "under-converting" relative to their own overall HE access levels. This will allow us to distinguish between groups that are excluded from the entire HE system versus those that enter HE but are systematically diverted away from high-tariff institutions. I propose mapping the **Residual Selectivity Gap**: the difference between a group's observed high-tariff rate and the rate predicted by a linear model of their overall HE progression. This will reveal if the "double hurdle" is a uniform phenomenon or if certain groups (e.g., specific ethnicities or SEN statuses) face a unique "institutional ceiling" that persists even when overall access gaps are controlled for.

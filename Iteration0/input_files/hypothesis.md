The "selectivity penalty"—the disparity in the conversion rate from any HE to high-tariff HE—is not uniform across demographic dimensions. I hypothesize that while area-based disadvantage (POLAR) shows a narrowing gap in overall access, the "selectivity penalty" remains static or widens for groups defined by systemic vulnerability (specifically SEN Status, Looked After Status, and Children in Need) compared to those defined by socioeconomic status (FSM/POLAR). By calculating the "Selectivity Elasticity" (the ratio of the high-tariff progression rate to the overall progression rate) for every category and comparing the slopes of these elasticities over time, we can identify whether the "glass ceiling" at selective institutions is a universal feature of the HE system or if it disproportionately affects specific marginalized groups regardless of their overall HE participation rates. This will reveal if policy interventions focusing on "any HE" access are inadvertently ignoring a deepening institutional stratification for the most vulnerable cohorts.

The "Selectivity Penalty" (the ratio of high-tariff progression to overall progression) is not uniform across all demographic dimensions, but rather exhibits a "compounding disadvantage" effect that varies by characteristic type. I hypothesize that while socioeconomic measures (POLAR, FSM) show a linear relationship between overall access and selectivity, identity-based or status-based measures (SEN, Looked After Status, Ethnicity) will demonstrate a "double-jeopardy" effect, where the selectivity penalty is disproportionately higher than what would be predicted by their overall HE progression rate alone. 

To test this, I propose calculating the "Selectivity Elasticity" for each breakdown category: the ratio of the high-tariff progression rate to the overall progression rate, normalized against the national average. By plotting these categories on a 2D plane (x-axis: Overall Progression Rate; y-axis: Selectivity Elasticity), we can identify which groups are "under-represented in elite pipelines" even when they achieve parity in "general HE access." This will reveal whether the barrier to high-tariff institutions is a function of general HE access or a specific, additional barrier to elite institutions that affects certain groups more severely than others, independent of their overall participation rate.
